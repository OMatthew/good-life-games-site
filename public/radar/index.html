HarrisRadar · project outline

Harris County, TX · Commercial & land

A commercial real estate opportunity radar for Harris County.

Pull the public record on all 1.5 million parcels in Harris County, score every one, and give you a dashboard that surfaces raw land and underbuilt sites worth a look. Filter it whenever you want, or get a short summary each week. Every parcel shows why it's there.

Why this helps

A lot of what makes a parcel interesting is already public: what the land is worth next to the building on it, the lot size, the age of any structure, who owns it and since when, whether it floods, what's been permitted nearby. The trouble is that it's spread across a dozen county and federal systems, and there are far more parcels than anyone can read through.

HarrisRadar pulls all of that into one place and scores every parcel. It won't tell you what to buy; that's your call. What it does is narrow 1.5 million parcels down to the few dozen worth a closer look.

Your confirmed starting screen

The first filter is now locked: raw or underbuilt land, 5 acres and up, with physical building footprint under 10% of the lot. Coverage means the building's footprint, not its appraised value.

That creates two working buckets. Vacant land stays in as 0% physical coverage. Improved land stays in when the existing building has a small footprint relative to the lot. Every row should be tagged so you can sort or filter the two opportunity types separately.

What each parcel comes with

The parcelExact site address, HCAD account number, legal description, lot size in acres, and a pin on the map.
OwnershipCurrent owner and mailing address, how long they've held it, and any recent change of hands.
ValueLand value, building value, total market value, value per acre, and how the building value stacks up against the land.
The buildingSize, year built, age, and footprint as a share of the lot (the coverage read).
TrendLast year's value and the year-over-year change, plus how the surrounding area is moving.
Risk & signalsFlood zone (100- and 500-year), nearby permit activity, tax delinquency, and foreclosure postings as those sources are added.
The scoreA score from 0 to 100, with a plain breakdown of which factors pushed it up or down.

The sample rows above are generic on purpose, so I'm not putting invented owners and addresses on screen.

How it works

1

Pull the record

Appraisal values, lot size, building size and age, ownership and how recently it changed, flood zone, recent permits nearby. Refreshed on a schedule.

2

Score every parcel

Value sitting in the land, an old or undersized building, a price out of step with the block, a recent ownership change, flood risk, nearby permit activity.

3

Give you a dashboard

A ranked, filterable list you can dig into anytime: narrow by area, size, flood, or price. Plus an optional weekly summary of what's new. You add what the data can't see: title issues, real access, what the block is actually like.

No black box. You can see exactly why each parcel scored the way it did, and tell me where it's off so I can tune it to you.

What I still need from you

The main scope question is answered. The remaining inputs mainly tune the ranking once the raw and underbuilt land screen is in place.

Quick confirms (defaults in parentheses)

  1. Property types. Commercial, industrial, multifamily, and vacant or raw land including rural acreage. Since raw land is in, single-family residential stays off by default unless you want builder lot inventory included.
  2. Geography. Whole county to start, or focus on certain submarkets or corridors first?
  3. What makes one a "good one"? Once a parcel clears 5 acres and low coverage, what should push it up the list? Frontage, out of the floodplain, a recent sale, price per acre, near new development? This is the most useful answer; it's what the ranking gets built on.
  4. Flood. Penalize floodplain parcels, or just show it as a column you judge? (I can do both.)
  5. How you'd use it. A dashboard you open and explore whenever you're sizing something up, a short summary emailed each week, or both? (I'm leaning both, with a spreadsheet export either way.)
  6. Update frequency. Refresh the underlying data weekly, or is monthly enough to start?
