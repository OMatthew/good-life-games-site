Why this helps

A lot of what makes a parcel interesting is already public: what the land is worth next to the building on it, the lot size, the age of any structure, who owns it and since when, whether it floods, what's been permitted nearby. The trouble is that it's spread across a dozen county and federal systems, and there are far more parcels than anyone can read through.

Opportunity Radar pulls all of that into one place and scores every commercial and land parcel. It won't tell you what to buy; that's your call. What it does is narrow 1.5 million parcels down to the few dozen worth a closer look.

Your initial parcel criteria

Your starting filter is set: raw or underbuilt land, 5 acres and up, with the building covering less than 10% of the lot. Coverage here means actual building footprint, not appraised value.

That gives two kinds of opportunity. Vacant land comes in at 0% coverage. Improved land comes in when the building on it is small next to the lot. Each one is tagged, so you can sort or filter the two types separately.

What each parcel comes with

The parcelExact site address, HCAD account number, legal description, lot size in acres, and a pin on the map.
OwnershipCurrent owner and mailing address, how long they've held it, and any recent change of hands.
ValueLand value, building value, total market value, value per acre, and how the building value stacks up against the land.
The buildingSize, year built, age, and footprint as a share of the lot (that's the coverage figure).
TrendLast year's value and the year-over-year change, plus how the surrounding area is moving.
Risk & signalsFlood zone (100- and 500-year), nearby permit activity, tax delinquency, and foreclosure postings as those sources are added.
The scoreA score from 0 to 100, with a plain breakdown of which factors pushed it up or down.

The sample rows above are generic on purpose, so I'm not putting invented owners and addresses on screen.

How it works

1

Pull the record

Appraisal values, lot size, building size and age, ownership and how recently it changed, flood zone, recent permits nearby. Refreshed on a schedule.

2

Score every parcel

Value sitting in the land, an old or undersized building, a price out of step with the block, a recent ownership change, flood risk, nearby permit activity.

3

Give you a dashboard

A ranked, filterable list you can dig into anytime: narrow by area, size, flood, or price. Plus an optional monthly summary of what's new. You add what the data can't see: title issues, real access, what the block is actually like.

No black box. You can see exactly why each parcel scored the way it did, and tell me where it's off so I can tune it to you. Down the line, the ranking also gets checked against 20 years of county history: which parcels like these actually got built on or outran their area, so the past can sharpen the ranking.

What I need from you now

The screen and the first ranking are built and running on real data. What I don't have yet is your judgment. The score's ingredients are still my educated guesses, and the fastest way to sharpen them is the three things below.

Three asks

  1. Mark up the first list. I'll send a short spreadsheet of the top of the ranking. How: tag your best ten and worst ten, with a few words on why each. Your words replace my guesses about what should push a parcel up or down.
  2. Give me a few deals you already know the answer to. Three to five you'd call winners, and a couple you'd call duds. Why: I'll look up where my score ranks them. If it ranks your winners high and your duds low, it's on track. Where it doesn't, the gap shows me exactly what to fix. This is the most useful thing you can give me.
  3. Your automatic "no" list. The owners, parcel shapes, or pockets of the county you'd never touch. Why: each one becomes a filter. I've already taught it to set aside HOA common areas, cemeteries, pipeline strips, and government land — yours extend that.
